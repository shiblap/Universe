H1 Heading

An H1 heading is the highest level in the HTML heading hierarchy. It is used for the main title of a webpage or a significant section, providing a clear indication of the primary topic. H1 headings are the most prominent and play a crucial role in SEO, accessibility, and content organization, helping users and search engines understand the main focus of the page.

H2 Heading

An H2 heading is the second level in the HTML heading hierarchy. It is used to mark major sections within a webpage, providing a clear structure and helping to organize content. H2 headings are typically used to denote primary subheadings under the main H1 heading, making them crucial for readability, accessibility, and SEO.

H3 Heading

An H3 heading is the third level in the HTML heading hierarchy. It is used to indicate subheadings within major sections, providing further breakdown of content. H3 headings are smaller and less prominent than H1 and H2 headings but are still important for organizing content and improving readability, accessibility, and SEO on a webpage.

H4 Heading

An H4 heading is the fourth level in the HTML heading hierarchy. It is used to denote subsections within a section, helping to organize content into a clear and structured format. H4 headings are smaller and less prominent than H1, H2, and H3 headings but still important for maintaining the logical flow and readability of a webpage. They contribute to better content organization, accessibility, and SEO.

H5 Heading

An H5 heading is the fifth level in the HTML heading hierarchy. It is used to indicate sub-subsections of content, providing additional structure within a webpage. H5 headings are styled to be smaller and less prominent than higher-level headings (H1-H4), but they still play an important role in organizing information and improving accessibility and SEO by defining the content's structure.

H6 Heading

An H6 heading is the smallest and least prominent heading tag in HTML. It is typically used to denote subsections or tertiary content within a webpage. H6 headings are often styled to be slightly smaller and lighter than higher-level headings, making them suitable for less significant content or supplementary information. While H6 headings still contribute to the hierarchical structure of a webpage, they carry less visual weight and are typically used sparingly to avoid cluttering the page.

CSS Grid supports advanced features such as subgrids, minmax(), and autofill/autofit functions, enabling more sophisticated layouts. Subgrids allow for nested grid layouts, inheriting the parent grid's track definitions. The minmax() function sets minimum and maximum sizes for grid tracks.

CSS Grid Layout empowers you to transform your creative vision into reality, delivering exceptional user experiences across the web. These functions help create responsive grids by automatically filling or fitting columns based on the available space.

Grid columns are a crucial component of CSS Grid Layout, enabling developers to create structured, flexible, and responsive web designs. By understanding and leveraging the full capabilities of grid columns, you can build complex layouts that adapt seamlessly to different devices and screen sizes.
